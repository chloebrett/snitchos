What the "UART hardware" physically is

There's no special connector — the UART is just three of the forty pins on that pin header (the same 0.1" header a Raspberry Pi has). Pin 1 is the corner nearest the board edge; count across in pairs.

board pin 6  GND  → adapter GND
board pin 8  TX   → adapter RX
board pin 10 RX   → adapter TX

TX and RX cross over — the board's "talk" wire goes to the adapter's "listen" wire.

The other half: a USB-serial adapter

A thumb-sized USB ↔ TTL dongle (CH340 / CP2102 / FTDI, ~$6). USB-A into your Mac on one end; three dupont jumper wires to the pins on the other. It turns the board's raw serial pins into a COM port your terminal opens at 115200.

⚠ must be 3.3 V logic

The header is 3.3 V-only. A 5 V adapter's data lines can damage the board — check the dongle has a 3.3 V jumper or is 3.3 V native.

The boot-mode switch — two tiny jumpers

RGPIO_1RGPIO_0Boots fromWhen you'd use it
LowLowQSPI flash← default: runs the U-Boot already in flash
LowHighmicroSDboot a distro image straight from SD
HighLoweMMCboot from an installed eMMC module
HighHighUARTone-time bootloader recovery / re-flash

For SnitchOS you leave it on QSPI flash — the board boots U-Boot, and U-Boot fetches your kernel over the network (TFTP). You only ever move these to High/High if you need to re-flash U-Boot itself.

So… is there a light we can flash?

Two different "flash." "Flash an SD card" / "boot from flash" means writing to flash memory (storage) — no light. And when I said the "blink" milestone, I was borrowing the embedded tradition where a new board's first sign of life is a blinked LED.

But on this board the power LED is hardwired, not software-controllable, and a genuinely blinkable LED would need a GPIO/pinmux driver SnitchOS doesn't have yet. The UART already works (it inherits OpenSBI's setup), so our real "first light" is the serial boot log, not a blinked LED — text on your terminal is the blink.
